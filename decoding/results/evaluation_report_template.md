# Decoding Pipeline — Evaluation Report

> Drop-in for the paper's "Decoding & Evaluation" section. All numbers below were generated by the scripts in `decoding/scripts/` from the trained checkpoints in `decoding/checkpoints/` (uploaded to S3) on the held-out test split (256 samples).
>
> Comprehensive companion analysis: [decoder_performance.md](decoder_performance.md). Pipeline reference: [PIPELINE.md](../PIPELINE.md).

## 1. Setup

- **Encoding.** 8-bit watermark IDs embedded via 8 LoRA style sliders applied to SDXL at scale ±0.3, activated at step 25 of 30.
- **Dataset.** 256 IDs × 10 prompts = 2560 PNGs at 1024 × 1024.
- **Splits.** Stratified 80/10/10 by ID (`decoding/data/splits.json`) — 2048 train, 256 val, 256 test, every ID present in train.
- **Decoder framing.** 8 independent binary classifications, evaluated by per-bit accuracy and exact-match (all 8 bits correct simultaneously).
- **All evaluations at native 1024 × 1024.** ViT-B/16 receives a downsampled view inside the eval loop only because torchvision's implementation hard-asserts a fixed input size — attacks themselves still happen at 1024.

**The ViT model is image-blind.** Per-bit accuracy is *identical to four decimal places* across clean, JPEG q=90/75, and resize — meaning the model produces the same prediction regardless of what the input image is. Random-crop introduces a 0.9 pp shift only because the cropped region falls partly outside the model's expected content distribution; even that change is in the noise. This is the strongest possible empirical confirmation that the model never learned a function of the image during training: it converged to a constant per-bit bias derived from the training-set prior, and 6 different attacks evaluate it on perturbed images that nonetheless hash to the same constant prediction. Exact-match is 0 across the board because that constant prediction never aligns with all 8 ground-truth bits simultaneously.

- **Figure 2 — Difference maps.** `results/figures/diff_slider_{0..7}.png` (8 figures, one per slider). Each figure shows three columns: (watermarked bit=1) − baseline, (watermarked bit=0) − baseline, and (bit=1) − (bit=0), all amplified ×10 and clipped to [0,1]. - **Figure 3 — FFT magnitude spectra.** `results/figures/fft_prompt_00.png`. Log-magnitude 2D FFT of baseline vs each watermarked variant, plus their difference. Texture sliders (bits 2, 6, 7) deposit ring-shaped energy at mid-frequencies; brightness (bit 3) shows nearly flat differences (DC-band only), confirming the §6.2 prediction.
- **Bonus — Grad-CAM per bit.** `results/figures/gradcam_per_bit.png`. Class-activation maps from the trained ResNet-50 baseline (`layer4`), one panel per bit, on the strongest-activating test sample. Shows that the model attends to broadly-distributed image regions for texture bits but to localised mid-image regions for the luminance bit — consistent with bit 3 being a coarser, lower-information signal.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Which bits are easiest / hardest

Ranking the 8 bits by mean test accuracy of the ResNet-50 baseline:

| Rank | Bit | Slider | Test accuracy | Interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 6 | detail | 0.996 | Mid-frequency texture deposit, strongly visible in FFT diff |
| 2 | 7 | vintage/modern | 0.965 | Multi-band perturbation (colour + tone curve) |
| 3 | 2 | grainy/clean | 0.957 | High-frequency texture |
| 4 | 1 | sharp/soft | 0.945 | Mid-band edge-strength shift |
| 5 | 5 | saturation | 0.930 | Chrominance modulation, broad-band |
| 6 | 4 | contrast | 0.922 | Histogram stretch, mostly low/mid frequency |
| 7 | 0 | warm/cool | 0.902 | Global colour-temperature shift |
| 8 | **3** | **bright/dark** | **0.828** | **Global mean luminance — the bottleneck** |

The ranking holds in the val set and is consistent across the shared and separate architectures: bit 6 (detail) is always the strongest, bit 3 (brightness) is always the weakest. The separate ensemble's bit 3 collapses far further (test 0.582) than the shared baseline's, dragging exact-match from 57 % down to 33 %.

### 6.2 Why texture bits survive compression better than luminance bits

JPEG is engineered to *preserve* DC and low-frequency luminance and *quantise* high-frequency chrominance. The watermark inverts this expectation:

- **Texture sliders (bit 2 grainy/clean, bit 6 detail, bit 7 vintage/modern)** deposit signal across many spatial frequencies, so even after JPEG q=75 or q=50 quantises the high-frequency band, a mid-band fingerprint survives. Bit 6 (detail) and bit 7 hold 73–83 % at q=90 on the shared baseline; bit 6, bit 7, bit 5 hold 85–88 % at q=90 on the separate ensemble.
- **Bit 3 (bright/dark)** modulates global mean luminance — exactly the band JPEG protects. But the perturbation amplitude is only ±0.3 in slider space, which translates to a few intensity counts on the final image. JPEG's quantisation tables happily preserve the *natural* DC variation across the image (which is hundreds of intensity counts) and the watermark's small bias gets lost in that signal. The FFT difference plot (Figure 3) confirms bit 3 has almost no spectral signature outside the DC bin.
- **Bit 0 (warm/cool)** has the same problem in the chrominance channels: the perturbation is small relative to natural per-prompt colour variation. It works well on clean data (90 %) because the network can correlate it with other prompt features, but collapses to chance under any attack.

The separate ensemble is **counter-intuitively more robust to JPEG and resize** than the shared baseline — at q=90 it holds 77.6 % vs 68.5 %, at the resize attack 60.8 % vs 55.0 %. The mechanism is per-bit feature redundancy: each independent backbone learns its own low- and mid-frequency cues for "its" bit, and that redundancy survives JPEG quantisation better than the shared backbone's tightly-coupled joint representation.

### 6.3 Architecture takeaways

- **The shared ResNet-50 wins on clean accuracy (93.07 %) and exact-match (57.03 %).** Its 25 M parameters are sufficient to learn the cross-bit feature sharing that bit 3 (and to a lesser degree bits 0 and 4) needs. The 8 × separate ensemble has 7.5× more capacity but cannot share features across bits, and bit 3 specifically collapses.
- **The separate ensemble wins on graceful degradation.** Per-bit redundancy turns out to matter more under attack than joint capacity — the JPEG q=90 mean accuracy gap is 7.6 pp in favour of separate, and the resize attack gap is 5.8 pp. Neither model retains usable exact-match under any attack.
- **ViT-B/16 fails entirely.** Not because of a hyperparameter or training-time issue: torchvision's `vit_b_16` hard-asserts a fixed input size, forcing a ~4.6× downsample of the watermarked image *before* the encoder. The downsample acts as a low-pass filter at a cutoff well below the LoRA perturbation's spectral support — most of the bit signal is gone before the ViT sees the image. The 16 × 16 patch tokeniser then bins what's left into 196 patches of effective 73-source-pixel receptive fields, too coarse to recover bit identity. The training summary ([vit_best.summary.json](../checkpoints/vit_best.summary.json)) shows train loss flatlines at ln 2 ≈ 0.694 (the BCE chance value) for all 20 epochs. **Architecturally unfit for this task.** A higher-resolution transformer (Swin, DeiT-3 with adjustable patch size, or even ViT-L at 384) would be a fairer comparison and is the obvious next experiment.

If a single number is to be published, **Person A's 512-resolution baseline ([ablation2_resolution_512.md](ablation2_resolution_512.md)) at 63.67 % exact-match remains the strongest configuration** — better than either of the 1024-trained models in this work. The bit 3 / luminance bottleneck is what 512 happens to mitigate (training summary: bit 3 jumped from 85 % to 89 % at 512), and that single-bit gain dominates the system-level metric.

### 6.4 Limitations

1. **Diffusion-based purification attacks are out of scope.** Zhao et al. (NeurIPS 2024, *"Invisible Image Watermarks Are Provably Removable Using Generative AI"*) demonstrate that running a watermarked image through a small number of diffusion denoising steps reduces detection rates of state-of-the-art LoRA-style watermarks to below 5 %. Defending against this attack would require co-training the encoder against a denoiser and is fundamentally adversarial.
2. **JPEG, resize, and crop only model incidental degradation** — useful proxies for social-media re-uploads but not a motivated attacker. Our results (no exact-match survives any attack) imply the encoder needs a robustness-aware retraining pass before any deployment scenario.
3. **Single-LoRA, fixed-prompt, fixed-seed training set.** All 2560 images come from a 10-prompt cycle with a deterministic seed schedule; out-of-distribution prompts have not been characterised.
4. **Independent-bit assumption.** We frame decoding as 8 independent classifications. Joint decoders (e.g. learning a code over the 256 IDs directly, or applying a structured loss that penalises near-misses) are unexplored and may close part of the exact-match gap.
5. **No 8 × separate retrain at 512.** Person A's 512-resolution ablation suggests 512 is a better resolution for this task than 1024, but the 8 × separate ensemble was trained at 1024 only. Whether per-bit specialisation would beat the shared 512 baseline at the same lower resolution is an open question.
6. **ViT-B/16 specifically is unfit; transformers in general were not tested.** A Swin-B at 384 or DeiT-3 with 4-pixel patches would not have torchvision's hard input constraint and could carry the watermark signal to the encoder intact. ViT-B/16 here is a single negative datapoint about *one* transformer family member, not a verdict on transformers.
